When teams compare die casting and CNC machining, the right answer depends on annual volume, geometry complexity, tolerance strategy, and launch timing.

This guide helps sourcing and product teams choose the process that minimizes total project risk.

![Die casting and CNC process planning](/img/engineering-team-reviewing-cad-models.webp "Choose process by total cost and launch plan")

## Quick Decision Rule

- Use CNC-first when quantity is low, geometry is still changing, and speed to first article is critical.
- Use die casting when annual demand can absorb tooling cost and repeatability matters in mass production.

## 1. Cost Structure Differences

Die casting has higher upfront tooling CAPEX but lower piece cost at scale. CNC has lower entry cost but higher recurring machining cost.

If your annual demand exceeds the break-even point, die casting usually wins on total landed cost.

## 2. Lead Time and Program Phase Fit

- CNC can ship prototypes quickly with minimal NRE.
- Die casting needs DFM, tooling build, T1/T2 optimization, and validation before SOP.

## 3. Tolerance and Surface Strategy

Complex parts often use a hybrid model:

- Near-net-shape die casting for efficiency.
- CNC post-machining on critical datums and sealing faces.

This model protects dimensional performance while controlling cycle cost.

## 4. Risk and Supply Chain Considerations

Key questions to ask before freezing the process:

- Is geometry stable enough for mold investment?
- Does the supplier control porosity and dimensional variation in serial production?
- Is quality validation (CMM/X-ray/leak test) defined for your risk class?
